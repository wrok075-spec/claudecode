# UGC CREATOR CHARACTER GENERATION REFERENCE
For generating the ChatGPT Images 2.0 prompt that creates the UGC creator who fronts the ad. The creator's face, vibe, and environment must match the brand's actual ICP — generic AI women lose the scroll.

================================================================
## THE FUNDAMENTAL RULE: MATCH THE CREATOR TO THE BUYER

The single biggest mistake in AI UGC right now is generating a generic-attractive-AI-woman in a generic-modern-apartment for every brand. Every UGC ad on Meta and TikTok using these defaults looks identical. The viewer registers "AI" instantly and scrolls.

The fix is specificity. The creator's age, vibe, environment, and energy should match the brand's actual ICP — the kind of person who'd plausibly buy and use the product in real life.

Before writing the character prompt, identify:

1. **Brand category** — wellness, beauty, food, beverage, supplements, apparel, etc.
2. **Customer archetype** — who actually buys this product? Age range, lifestyle, vibe, where they live emotionally
3. **Customer's ritual/use moment** — when and where do they use the product? That's the environment.
4. **Vibe spectrum** — is the brand aspirational? Approachable? Performance-oriented? Calm? The creator's energy should match.

If the brief doesn't tell you the ICP, ask one focused question before generating the prompt: "Who actually buys this product?" Don't default to "26-year-old woman in modern apartment."

## CREATOR ARCHETYPE LIBRARY

Six common DTC creator archetypes. Each pairs with categories where it converts. Use these as starting points and modify based on the specific brand.

### Archetype 1: The Wellness Mom (32-42)
- **Age range**: 32-42
- **Vibe**: Calm, present, "has her routine," warm but not bubbly
- **Wardrobe**: Oatmeal/cream knit tops, neutral sweatpants, minimal jewelry, hair half-up or low ponytail with face-framing pieces loose, no makeup or "glowy minimal"
- **Environment**: Apartment kitchen with lived-in details (cookbooks, herbs, a plant, ceramic mugs, a kettle), or a tidy home office
- **Lighting**: Soft natural daylight, warm-cool tones
- **Best for**: Wellness supplements (AG1, Ritual), clean beauty (Beautycounter), high-end pantry brands (Brightland), parenting-adjacent products

### Archetype 2: The Downtown Girl (24-29)
- **Age range**: 24-29
- **Vibe**: Aspirational, slightly "off-duty model," confident, calm-cool energy
- **Wardrobe**: Fitted white tank or slip-style camisole, no jewelry or minimal, hair down with soft natural waves, glossy lip, dewy skin, glowy makeup
- **Environment**: Bathroom vanity with a mirror, a cluster of unbranded skincare bottles, ceramic dish, or bedroom with morning light
- **Lighting**: Soft natural daylight, slightly cool tones, glowy and minimal
- **Best for**: Beauty (Rhode, Glossier), skincare (Bubble, Topicals), luxury self-care, fragrance

### Archetype 3: The Desk Worker (27-32)
- **Age range**: 27-32
- **Vibe**: Tired-but-warm, self-deprecating, "I just got home from work"
- **Wardrobe**: Oversized cream/beige knit sweater, casual loungewear, hair in a messy half-up clip, minimal makeup, slight under-eye-tired look
- **Environment**: Apartment kitchen counter, home office, couch — lived-in spaces with mugs, laptop, notebooks, real clutter
- **Lighting**: Late afternoon warm window light, slightly hazy
- **Best for**: Snacks (Barebells, Magic Spoon), coffee/beverages (Olipop, Liquid Death), productivity adjacent products, anything pitched on "energy" or "stress"

### Archetype 4: The Active Adult (28-36)
- **Age range**: 28-36
- **Vibe**: Confident, healthy, "I work out but I'm not a fitness influencer"
- **Wardrobe**: Athletic zip-up over white tank, leggings, hair pulled back in higher ponytail, slight flush, no makeup, sweat or post-workout look
- **Environment**: Outdoor walk, gym (briefly), kitchen post-workout, yoga studio
- **Lighting**: Bright natural daylight, slight warmth, real outdoor or gym light
- **Best for**: Hydration brands (Liquid I.V., Element), protein products, athletic apparel, recovery products

### Archetype 5: The Creative Professional (29-36)
- **Age range**: 29-36
- **Vibe**: Sharp, intentional, slightly artsy, considered
- **Wardrobe**: Linen button-down or fitted neutral knit, structured pieces, hair in a clean low bun or curls, minimal-but-deliberate makeup, statement jewelry
- **Environment**: Home office with art on walls, design studio, well-styled apartment, coffee shop with notebook
- **Lighting**: Even natural daylight, gallery-clean tones
- **Best for**: Premium home goods (Our Place, Brightland), high-end skincare, design-adjacent products, premium pantry brands

### Archetype 6: The Suburban Dad (35-45)
- **Age range**: 35-45
- **Vibe**: Easygoing, dry-humor, real-talk, no-nonsense
- **Wardrobe**: T-shirt or henley, jeans or shorts, scruff, simple watch, no styling
- **Environment**: Garage, backyard, kitchen, car interior, hardware store
- **Lighting**: Mixed natural and overhead, slightly more cinematic
- **Best for**: Tools, home goods, grilling/BBQ products, men's grooming, supplements pitched at men, family-oriented products

## THE CHARACTER PROMPT TEMPLATE

Use this template. Fill in every bracket with brand-specific specificity. The more specific you are, the less generic the output.

```
Generate a candid iPhone-style photo of a [AGE]-year-old [GENDER] standing in [SPECIFIC ENVIRONMENT] on a [TIME OF DAY]. [EXPRESSION DESCRIPTION], looking directly at the camera at eye level. Wearing [WARDROBE — specific items, casual, real, lived-in], [HAIR STYLE — specific, real-feeling], [MAKEUP NOTE — minimal, glowy, none, etc.]. [LIGHTING DESCRIPTION] from camera-left, no direct sun. The [ENVIRONMENT] is real and lived-in — [3-4 SPECIFIC PROPS that anchor the space — name actual items, not categories]. Subject is centered slightly off-axis to the right of frame, hands resting near her waist or near the counter or near her face. Shallow depth of field, slight handheld phone-style framing, no pose, no professional lighting, no studio look. Color palette is [WARM/MUTED/NATURAL TONES specific to the brand vibe]. Aspect ratio 9:16 vertical. Photo feels like she just took a selfie because something happened.
```

## THE SPECIFICITY RULES

These distinguish a creator that looks real from one that looks AI-generated:

### Wardrobe specificity
- ❌ "Casual top and pants"
- ✅ "Fitted oatmeal-colored long-sleeve top, dark gray drawstring sweatpants"

### Hair specificity
- ❌ "Hair down"
- ✅ "Hair in a low ponytail with a few face-framing pieces loose"

### Environment specificity
- ❌ "Modern kitchen"
- ✅ "Apartment kitchen with a marble counter, a half-full mug of coffee, a small potted plant, a wooden cutting board leaning against the backsplash, soft blurred shelving with a ceramic mug and a small cookbook"

### Lighting specificity
- ❌ "Natural light"
- ✅ "Late afternoon natural light from a window camera-left, warm and slightly hazy, no direct sun"

### Vibe specificity
- ❌ "Smiling at camera"
- ✅ "Tired-but-warm expression, slight half-smile, looking directly at the camera at eye level"

## ITERATION GUIDANCE

ChatGPT Images 2.0 doesn't give you 4 variations at once like dedicated image tools. Iterate via follow-up messages:

- "Make her hair looser"
- "More clutter on the counter"
- "Less posed expression"
- "Younger" or "older"
- "More wardrobe specificity — change the top to a cream knit cardigan"

Treat ChatGPT like a photographer you're directing in real time. The first generation gets you 80% there. Two or three follow-up tweaks get you to lockable.

What to look for in the final image:
- Hands don't look melted or have extra fingers
- Eyes aren't glassy or doll-like
- Skin texture has natural pores, freckles, slight asymmetry
- Environment looks lived-in, not styled
- The face would not stop you on a TikTok scroll thinking "AI"

If after 4-5 iterations the image still looks AI-generic, regenerate from scratch with even more specificity in the prompt.

## SAVING AS A CHARACTER ELEMENT

Once the image is locked, save it as a Higgsfield Character Element:

1. New Element → upload the image
2. Set Category to **Character**
3. Name as `@creator_[brand_name]` or descriptive handle
4. Add a description that locks face/hair/wardrobe across scenes:

For single-setting ads:
```
[AGE]-year-old [GENDER] with [HAIR DESCRIPTION], [WARDROBE SUMMARY], [SKIN/MAKEUP NOTE], [EXPRESSION ANCHOR]. Face, hair, and wardrobe remain identical across every scene.
```

For multi-environment ads (where wardrobe will change between scenes):
```
[AGE]-year-old [GENDER] with [HAIR DESCRIPTION], [BASE WARDROBE], [SKIN/MAKEUP NOTE], [EXPRESSION ANCHOR]. Face, hair, and core wardrobe remain identical across every scene. Specific wardrobe items can change between scenes to show time-of-day progression.
```

The description has a character limit in Higgsfield (~200 characters in practice). Keep it tight. The face anchor is the most important part — describe what makes her face distinctive, not what she's wearing.

================================================================
## MULTI-CREATOR AD SETUP

For multi-creator dialogue ads, generate two distinct creators with deliberate contrast:

- Different ages (e.g., 26 and 32)
- Different hair colors (one brunette, one blonde, or one curly, one straight)
- Different energy archetypes (one calm, one excited; one expert, one novice)
- Different wardrobe palettes (one warm-toned, one cool-toned)

If both creators look too similar, the viewer can't distinguish them in dialogue cuts and the ad confuses. Force differentiation in the character prompts.

Save each as a separate Character Element (`@creator_a`, `@creator_b`).
