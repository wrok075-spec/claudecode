# ACTION SHOT UGC REFERENCE
For UGC ads where the creator physically interacts with the product in a meaningful way — pouring, applying, scooping, mixing, demonstrating. The action is the proof.

================================================================
## WHEN TO USE ACTION SHOTS

Action shots are mandatory for product categories where the *experience of using the product* is the conversion driver. The viewer needs to see the bottle pour, the cream go on, the powder scoop, the spray hit the skin. Telling beats listing, and showing beats telling.

Use action shots when:

- The product has a sensory or visual experience (beverages pouring, foaming, fizzing)
- The application process is part of the value (skincare, makeup, body products)
- The product format is novel (squeeze bottles, dispensers, pump mechanisms)
- The transformation is visible (before/after, color change, texture change)
- The brand is positioned around ritual or care (the slow pour, the careful application)

Do NOT rely on action shots when:

- The product is consumed silently (a swallowed pill, a worn item)
- The action is too subtle to read on a 9:16 mobile screen
- Showing the action would require unwrapping the product (high-failure-mode in Seedance — see "Eating and Unwrapping" below)

================================================================
## THE CORE PRINCIPLE: ACTION = CAMERA CHOICE

The single most important rule: **the camera angle determines whether an action shot works.**

Selfie-angle handheld is wrong for most action shots. The creator's face is the focus and her hands are at the bottom of frame. The product action gets cropped or hidden.

For action shots, switch to one of three camera modes:

**Mode 1 — Over-shoulder demo angle**
Camera is behind/beside the creator, looking past her shoulder at what her hands are doing. The product is the visual subject. The creator's face is partially or not visible. This is the YouTuber kitchen demo angle.

**Mode 2 — Close-hold product reveal (chest-up)**
Camera is selfie-angle but zoomed slightly in. The creator's chest and hands are in frame. Her face is visible at the top of frame. The product action happens in the lower-middle. This is the talking-head-with-product angle.

**Mode 3 — POV first-person**
Camera is positioned as if it IS the creator's eyes. Her hands enter from the bottom of frame. The product moves. Her face is not visible at all. This is the immersive demo angle.

Choosing the wrong mode is the most common reason action shots fail. Match the mode to the action:

- **Pouring a beverage**: over-shoulder demo or POV
- **Applying skincare**: close-hold (so the face shows the application) or POV
- **Scooping powder**: over-shoulder demo
- **Spraying**: close-hold (face shows reaction) or POV
- **Mixing/stirring**: over-shoulder demo
- **Unboxing**: POV first-person

• Delivery: warm casual close, slight nod

### THE CRITICAL CONSISTENCY RULES

1. **Camera mode is per-scene, not per-ad.** Don't lock to selfie-angle for the whole ad. The action scenes need the right angle. Mode-shift between scenes — that's part of why the action lands.

2. **The action gets a dedicated scene.** Don't try to talk and pour at the same time in one shot. Seedance handles single actions cleanly; layered actions break. Pour scene = no dialogue. Talking scene = no pouring.

3. **The ambient sound IS the soundtrack of the action scene.** No music, no voiceover. The fizz, the pour, the click of ice — these are real-feel cues that signal authenticity.

4. **Reaction shot must follow the action shot.** The pour means nothing without the sip + reaction. Always pair an action with a reaction in the next scene.

5. **The product must be unmistakably brand-accurate during the action.** This is the high-failure-mode moment. The label can morph, the color can shift, the form factor can warp. Use a saved Prop Element with detailed wrapper/can/bottle description.

## PROMPT STRUCTURE FOR ACTION SCENES

For an over-shoulder pour shot:

```
Vertical 9:16 video, [DURATION] seconds, single continuous handheld shot. CAMERA ANGLE: over-shoulder demo angle — camera is positioned behind and slightly to the right of @creator_d, looking past her right shoulder down at the kitchen counter. Her face is partially visible (right cheek, ear, hair) but the focus is on her hands and the action.

ENVIRONMENT: [SPECIFIC KITCHEN/LOCATION DETAILS — counter material, props in frame, lighting].

ACTION (00:00–[DURATION]): Her right hand is holding @[product] at chest height. She slowly tilts the [can/bottle] toward a [glass/tumbler/container] also visible on the counter. The [liquid/contents] pours steadily — describe what the pour looks like (foam, fizz, color, splash pattern). The pour fills the container. She sets the [can/bottle] down naturally. Brief lens focus hunt as she completes the action.

NO DIALOGUE in this scene. Audio: ambient kitchen sound, audible pour, [SPECIFIC AUDIO CUES — fizz, ice clinking, splash].

Camera: handheld phone, natural micro-wobble, no zooms, no cuts. The @[product] [wrapper/can/bottle] must remain photorealistic and brand-accurate throughout. Native UGC quality, looks like an iPhone video.
```

For a close-hold application shot (skincare):

```
Vertical 9:16 selfie-style video, [DURATION] seconds, single continuous handheld shot. CAMERA ANGLE: close-hold selfie — camera is selfie-angle but framed chest-up, so @creator_b's face is at the top of frame and her hands and the product action are in the lower-middle of frame.

ENVIRONMENT: Bathroom or vanity setting, soft natural light, mirror partially visible, shelf of unbranded products in soft focus.

ACTION (00:00–[DURATION]): @creator_b is holding @[product] in her right hand near chest level. She [PUMPS / SQUEEZES / TILTS] the bottle, dispensing a small amount onto her left fingertips. She brings her fingers to her face and applies the product to her [CHEEK / FOREHEAD / SPECIFIC AREA] in a gentle [PATTING / SMOOTHING / CIRCULAR] motion. Her face shows a small relaxed expression as she applies — eyes briefly closed, slight smile. The product texture is visible on her skin briefly before absorbing.

[OPTIONAL DIALOGUE during application — usually voiceover-style as she's looking down at her face]

Camera: handheld phone, natural micro-wobble, no zooms, no cuts. The @[product] container must remain photorealistic and brand-accurate. Native UGC quality.
```

For a POV first-person shot:

```
Vertical 9:16 first-person POV video, [DURATION] seconds, single continuous handheld shot. CAMERA ANGLE: POV first-person — camera is positioned as if it IS @creator's eyes, looking down at her hands on a [SURFACE/COUNTER]. Her face is NOT visible. Only her hands and forearms enter from the bottom of frame.

ENVIRONMENT: [VIEWED FROM HER PERSPECTIVE — what she sees in front of her].

ACTION (00:00–[DURATION]): Her hands enter from the bottom of frame holding @[product]. She [PERFORMS THE ACTION — describe in physical detail]. The product moves [HOW IT MOVES]. Her hands complete the action and either rest or exit frame.

NO DIALOGUE — voiceover only if needed, but POV scenes work best as ambient action.

Camera: handheld phone, natural micro-wobble, slight POV head-motion bob, no zooms. The @[product] must remain brand-accurate. Audio: ambient sound of the action. Native UGC quality.
```

## EATING AND UNWRAPPING — THE WARNING

Two specific actions are HIGH-FAILURE-MODE in Seedance UGC right now:

**Eating shots.** The chew motion looks uncanny. The product morphs mid-bite. The wrapper teleports or vanishes.

**Unwrapping shots.** The wrapper folds in unrealistic ways. The transition from sealed → opened often produces mangled output. The exposed product looks generic if no unwrapped reference image exists.

If your script demands eating or unwrapping:

1. **First, ask if you can avoid it.** Hold-and-explain frames work just as well for most narratives. The dialogue can reference the experience without performing it.
2. **If you must do it, save TWO product references.** One sealed image, one unwrapped/exposed image. Add both to the same Element with description noting both states.
3. **Budget extra regenerations.** Plan for 4-6 generations to land a clean eating or unwrapping shot.
4. **Have a fallback ready.** If 6 generations don't work, rewrite the script to avoid the action.

## SOUND DESIGN FOR ACTION SHOTS

Seedance generates audio with the video. For action shots, the AMBIENT SOUND of the action is what sells authenticity. Always describe the expected audio in your prompt:

- Pour: "audible pour, liquid hitting glass, fizz rising"
- Spray: "audible spray sound, brief mist hiss"
- Application: "near-silent, soft skin-contact sound, ambient room tone"
- Scoop: "powder scooping sound, container tap as scoop knocks side"
- Mix: "stirring sound, spoon hitting glass, contents swirling"

If Seedance generates the action without the expected audio, the scene falls flat. Be explicit.

================================================================
## COMMON FAILURE MODES

1. **Wrong camera angle for the action.** Selfie-angle for a pour = action gets cropped. Always match camera mode to action.

2. **Layered actions.** Talking + pouring = both fail. Separate them into distinct scenes.

3. **Product distortion during the action.** The label morphs, the bottle shape changes, the color shifts. Use a saved Prop Element with thorough description.

4. **Missing reaction shot.** An action without a reaction is noise. Always follow an action with a reaction beat (the sip, the post-application skin-look, the post-mix taste).

5. **Action too fast for the duration.** Pouring a full glass in 2 seconds looks rushed. Allow 4-6 seconds for the action itself, plus padding.

6. **Trying to make eating shots work.** They usually don't. Rewrite the script.
